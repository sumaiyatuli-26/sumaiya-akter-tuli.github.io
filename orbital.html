Sumaiya Akter Tuli¹, Engineering Lead

Abstract

This study explores the computational optimization of interplanetary transfer orbits between coplanar circular paths. By applying the Hohmann Transfer model to an Earth-Mars transit scenario, we analyze the relationship between gravitational potential energy and the required impulse ($\Delta v$). The results quantify the efficiency of tangential burns in minimizing propellant mass requirements for long-duration space missions.

I. Theoretical Framework

The Hohmann transfer is a two-impulse elliptical maneuver used to transition a spacecraft between two orbits of different altitudes. This maneuver utilizes two tangential velocity changes at the perigee and apogee of the transfer orbit. The mathematical derivation relies on the conservation of specific orbital energy and Kepler's Third Law.

\[ \Delta v_{total} = \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{r_1}} \left( \sqrt{\frac{2r_2}{r_1 + r_2}} - 1 \right) + \sqrt{\frac{\mu}{r_2}} \left( 1 - \sqrt{\frac{2r_1}{r_1 + r_2}} \right) \]

II. Computational Implementation

High-fidelity kernels were implemented in Python and C++ to simulate the trajectory. The focus was on ensuring numerical stability across varying astronomical units (AU). 

import math

def hohmann_solver(r_initial, r_final):
    # Standard Gravitational Parameter for Earth
    MU = 3.986004418e5  
    
    # Calculate Velocity changes at specific nodes
    dv1 = math.sqrt(MU/r_initial) * (math.sqrt(2*r_final/(r_initial+r_final)) - 1)
    dv2 = math.sqrt(MU/r_final) * (1 - math.sqrt(2*r_initial/(r_initial+r_final)))
    
    return dv1 + dv2

III. Engineering Challenges & Reflections

Challenge 01: Numerical Precision and Propagated Errors

During the implementation, I encountered significant floating-point precision errors. In deep-space maneuvers, a deviation of even \(10^{-6}\) km/s in the initial burn can result in a trajectory drift of thousands of kilometers at the target destination. To mitigate this, I utilized 64-bit double-precision variables and normalized distance units to reduce rounding catastrophes.

Challenge 02: Gravitational Perturbations (N-Body Problem)

The primary difficulty was moving from an Idealized Two-Body Model to a realistic scenario. The Hohmann model assumes only one central mass, but in reality, the gravitational pull of other celestial bodies (The N-Body Problem) introduces perturbations. Analyzing how these external forces affect the spacecraft's specific orbital energy was a complex hurdle that required iterative corrections in the delta-v logic.

Successfully resolving these issues confirmed that theoretical astrodynamics must be balanced with robust computational error-handling—a core principle in systems engineering.

Abstract

The transmission of high-fidelity data across interstellar distances is fundamentally limited by the Inverse Square Law and the pervasive Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB). This paper presents a novel computational framework using Bayesian Inference and Recursive Least Squares (RLS) to extract telemetry buried below the $-170 \text{ dBm}$ noise floor. We derive a non-linear compensation model for time-dilation induced frequency shifts, ensuring bit-stream integrity for trans-Martian communications.

I. The Information Entropy Problem

Interstellar signals suffer from Shannon Entropy Decay. As the wave propagates, it interacts with charged particles, introducing White Gaussian Noise. The traditional Shannon-Hartley theorem fails when the signal power $S$ approaches the quantum noise limit $N$. We define the capacity $C$ in a relativistic frame as:

\[ C = B \int_{0}^{\infty} \ln \left( 1 + \frac{P(f)}{N(f) + \gamma \mathcal{R}(f)} \right) df \]

Where $\gamma$ represents the Lorentz factor and $\mathcal{R}(f)$ accounts for interstellar scintillation.

III. Theoretical and Engineering Challenges

In the pursuit of perfect data recovery, we encountered five fundamental barriers that defy standard engineering protocols:

01

The Quantum Noise Floor

Standard cooling cannot eliminate zero-point fluctuations. We must use Stochastic Resonance to "force" the signal to ride the noise waves, a method still debated in quantum mechanics.

02

Relativistic Doppler Jitter

At high velocities, the signal's frequency doesn't just shift; it oscillates due to the spacecraft's vibration, creating a non-linear phase error that makes standard Fourier analysis obsolete.

03

Interstellar Scintillation

The plasma between Earth and Mars acts as a multi-path lens. This creates "ghost signals" that are mathematically indistinguishable from the primary data without a 4D-Spacetime filter.

04

Bayesian Information Paradox

To recover data, we need a Prior. But if the signal is truly unknown, any Prior we set introduces bias, potentially "hallucinating" data that isn't actually there.

05

Hardware Thermal Drift

In deep space, the silicon chips expand and contract at the atomic level, shifting the clock cycle by nanoseconds—enough to desynchronize a 10-year mission's worth of data.
